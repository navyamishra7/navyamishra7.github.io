Navya Mishra

PhD Candidate · Neural Interfaces · Electrophysiology · Neurotechnology · Wearable Biosensors · Bioelectronics

My research focuses on building neural interface systems that bridge the gap between engineered devices and living neural tissue. I develop scalable 3D microelectrode array (MEA) platforms for high-throughput, long-term electrophysiological recording in human brain organoids, enabling quantitative characterization of disease-specific neural phenotypes at scale — including across 70+ samples in Angelman syndrome models.

I am especially interested in translational neurotechnology — designing devices and experimental frameworks that can move from the bench to the clinic. My work spans neural data analysis (automated spike detection, feature extraction, and statistical pipelines), wearable and biohybrid biosensors, and electroactive bioelectronic systems for wound care and molecular computation at the interface of biology and electronics.

I received my PhD (expected 2026) in Electrical Engineering from North Carolina State University, advised by Albert Keung and Amay Bandodkar, with dual undergraduate degrees (B.E. + M.Sc.) from BITS Pilani, India.

Research Focus

My work spans neural interface engineering, large-scale electrophysiology, and clinically translatable bioelectronic systems — from device fabrication to data analysis to disease modeling.

Neural Interface Engineering

Design and fabrication of scalable 3D microelectrode array (MEA) platforms for long-term, high-throughput electrophysiological recording in human brain organoids, with cost-efficient nanomaterial electrode processes.

Large-Scale Electrophysiology

Automated MATLAB/Python pipelines for spike detection, feature extraction, and statistical analysis of neural time-series data, enabling quantitative comparison of neural activity across experimental conditions at scale.

Disease Modeling & Neurotechnology

Characterizing disease-specific neural phenotypes using iPSC-derived brain organoid models — including electrophysiological evidence of Angelman syndrome — to advance translational neurotechnology applications.

Wearable Biosensors

Soft, wearable microfluidic sensing systems with finger-actuated pumps and valves for on-demand, longitudinal, multi-analyte sweat analysis — enabling continuous health monitoring without batteries.

Bioelectronic Systems

Clinically translatable bioelectronic devices including water-powered, electronics-free wound-care dressings that generate electrical stimulation for accelerated healing, and biohybrid DNA logic sensors.

Electrochemistry & Nanomaterials

Nanomaterial synthesis and electrochemical characterization (CV, EIS, chronoamperometry) for next-generation biosensors and neural probes, achieving 15× cost reduction over noble metal electrodes.
